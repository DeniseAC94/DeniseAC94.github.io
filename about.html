About

Evolutionary biology, morphology, methods and science communication.

I’m Denise Crampton, an evolutionary biology PhD researcher, geometric morphometrics specialist, and science communicator. My work focuses on biological shape, morphological variation, fossils, evolutionary patterns, and the practical methods used to study form across living and extinct organisms.

Research background

I am completing a self-funded PhD in Evolutionary Biology at Liverpool John Moores University. My project investigates morphological variation in mammals and ecogeographical patterns revealed through geometric morphometrics.

My research background spans mammals, reptiles, fishes, fossils, and zooarchaeological material, including work on Carnivora skull morphology, insectivorous bat mandibles, island lizard morphology, East African cichlid taxonomy, fossil and extant bear ecology, isotope analysis, and landmark-based methods.

I have held visiting research roles at Sapienza University of Rome and the Muséum national d’Histoire naturelle in Paris, working on morphology, geometric morphometrics, and evolutionary biology projects.
